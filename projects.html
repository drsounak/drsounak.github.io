Why Does Tumour Growth Stall? The Role of Avascular Phases in Cancer Progression

Before a tumour can grow beyond 1–2mm in diameter, it faces a fundamental constraint: without a blood supply, oxygen and nutrients must diffuse in from the surrounding tissue. This diffusion limit is why avascular tumour growth eventually stalls — and why modelling this phase mathematically tells us so much about cancer behaviour. In this post, I discuss the mathematics behind this stalling and what our anomalous diffusion models revealed.

Fractals in Biology: How Tumour Blood Vessel Networks Defy Euclidean Geometry

The branching, chaotic network of blood vessels that a tumour builds for itself cannot be described by smooth, Euclidean shapes — its fractal dimension tells a richer story. In this post, I explain what fractal dimension means in a biological context, how we measured it using the box-counting method in our cellular automata model of tumour angiogenesis, and why the fractal properties of tumour vasculature matter for drug delivery and treatment planning.

Feature Selection in Biomedical Data: A Practical Guide to Mutual Information Methods

High-dimensional biomedical datasets — genomics, proteomics, clinical records — are plagued by irrelevant and redundant features that degrade model performance. Mutual information (MI) measures how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about another, making it a powerful, model-agnostic feature relevance criterion. This post walks through the hybrid MI-based framework we developed and why clustering was the key to eliminating redundancy without sacrificing relevant features.
